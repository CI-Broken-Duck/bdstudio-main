# Why I Chose Chiang Mai to Launch My AI Startup

## Building from Outside the Bubble

Over the past 10 years, I’ve lived and worked in China, Canada, Vietnam, and Malaysia—building education platforms, running video teams, and launching software tools used by millions of learners. I’ve created content in multiple languages, led international productions, and more recently, developed AI-first applications in areas like music generation and test preparation.

I’m not chasing funding rounds or trying to play the Silicon Valley game. What I needed was time, focus, and a low-burn environment to build real tools. That’s what led me to Chiang Mai.

People often ask: *Why Thailand? Why Chiang Mai?*

This article breaks it down. If you're a founder working remotely, especially in software or creative tech, Chiang Mai might offer more than you'd expect—without the usual overhead.

## The Infrastructure Is Quietly World-Class

### Reliable High-Speed Internet

One of the first things I checked before committing to Chiang Mai was the internet situation. It’s easy to assume Southeast Asia means unreliable speeds—but that’s not the case here. In most central neighborhoods like Nimmanhaemin and Santitham, you can get fiber plans up to 1 Gbps for under ฿1,000 a month. Most condos also come with pre-installed routers and backup SIM card failover options.

Personally, I run frequent uploads of large AI-generated media files—MIDI sequences, SoundFont packs, vector datasets—and take daily calls with clients and collaborators in North America and Europe. I’ve had fewer outages here than I did in Toronto.

For mobile, 5G coverage is consistent across the city, and SIM packages with unlimited data are cheap. When the fiber did go down once during a storm, I tethered to my phone and continued streaming HD calls without issue.

### Chiang Mai International Airport

Chiang Mai’s airport isn’t massive, but it’s surprisingly efficient. You can fly direct to Bangkok in about an hour, and from there connect to virtually any major international hub. There are also direct flights to Singapore, Kuala Lumpur, Seoul, and seasonal routes to cities in China, Japan, and Vietnam.

This matters when you're running a global-facing business. Whether you’re flying out for a conference, bringing in a collaborator, or just need a visa run, travel is quick and painless compared to cities with more congested hubs.

## Operating Costs Are Founder-Friendly

### Low Personal Rent

I rent a small standalone house in Pong Noi for ฿5,000 a month—about $135 USD. It’s quiet, surrounded by greenery, and gives me the space to work and live without distraction. I added a fiber line for another ฿500/month, and even with water and electricity, I rarely spend more than ฿7,000 total.

There’s no comparison with what I’d pay in Toronto, London, or even Hanoi. A one-bedroom condo in any of those cities would easily cost $1,000–$2,000 USD per month, often without utilities, internet, or outdoor space. Here, I get privacy, a stable connection, and the headspace to actually build. The money I save goes straight back into development and growth.

### Low Commercial and Office Costs

If you prefer a structured work environment, Chiang Mai offers some of the best value in Asia. A hot desk at Yellow Coworking costs around ฿3,500/month (~$95 USD) with 24/7 access and fast fiber. Punspace, with locations in Nimman and Tha Pae, provides quieter shared spaces and good networking opportunities.

For those running small teams or needing something more permanent, you can lease a full office or shophouse for ฿8,000–฿12,000 per month. Many founders choose locations near Chiang Mai University’s Science Park, where you’re close to interns, researchers, and talent from STEM programs.

There’s no long-term lock-in, no hidden charges, and no pressure to scale office space prematurely. You only pay for what you actually need—and that flexibility helps early-stage teams stay focused.

## Western Amenities Without Western Prices

Chiang Mai isn’t a tradeoff—it’s an upgrade in many ways. The city offers all the amenities Western founders expect: high-end coffee shops, clean and modern hospitals, imported groceries, gyms, delivery apps, and international dining. But the prices are 30–70% lower than what you’d pay in most Western cities.

You can have brunch in a Scandinavian-style cafe, get a dental cleaning in a private clinic, or work out in an air-conditioned gym with all the equipment you’d expect—at a fraction of the cost you'd pay in New York or London. Imported goods are widely available in supermarkets like Rimping, and local produce is abundant and cheap.

There’s no sacrifice in quality of life here. In fact, most remote workers I’ve met report their standard of living went up after relocating. You’re not just saving money—you’re getting more for what you spend.

For Western founders or remote-first teams, the transition is seamless. English is spoken in most service environments, there’s strong infrastructure for digital payments and banking, and the lifestyle supports long-term focus. Chiang Mai doesn’t just make it possible to build lean—it makes it sustainable.

## A Tech Scene That's Small but Smart

### Digital Nomads and Remote Tech Workers

Chiang Mai is often called the digital nomad capital of Asia, and for good reason. The city attracts thousands of remote workers every year—designers, developers, marketers, and plenty of people who just want to say they’re “building something.”

To be blunt: not all nomads are serious operators. There are a lot of life coaches, passive income gurus, crypto speculators, and burned-out backpackers trying to rebrand as founders. Many are unemployed, overconfident, and not actually building anything of substance.

That said, if you cut through the noise, there’s a strong core of remote workers doing real work—freelancers, agency owners, SaaS founders, and bootstrappers. I’ve had great conversations and met thoughtful people at coworking hubs like Yellow, HeartWork, and Mana. The nomad crowd may be inconsistent, but the ecosystem around it—meetups, talks, and casual coworking collisions—can be useful if you know what you’re looking for.

### Startups, Expats, and Bootstrappers

Beyond the drop-in nomads, there’s a quieter but more committed layer of expats and founders who actually live here long term. Many run agencies, niche SaaS products, e-learning platforms, or media businesses. You won’t see them posting in Facebook groups—they’re too busy working—but they’re here.

I found collaborators, developers, and even legal/accounting support just by talking to people in the right circles. Most aren’t raising money or chasing headlines—they’re building solid businesses under the radar. It’s a refreshing contrast to the overhyped culture of Silicon Valley.

Chiang Mai’s tech scene isn’t huge, but it’s efficient. If you’re building something real and not just playing founder dress-up, you’ll find others doing the same.

## Chiang Mai University and the Language Bridge

One of the more underrated parts of being based in Chiang Mai is the access to local talent—especially through Chiang Mai University (CMU). The university has a strong science and engineering faculty, and its Science and Technology Park is home to several small labs, incubators, and research groups that welcome outside collaboration.

I’ve worked with interns and freelancers from CMU on everything from frontend dev to audio annotation. Their technical skills are solid, and English proficiency—especially in the Nimman area—is far higher than in most parts of Thailand.

Being near the university also means access to meetups, seminars, and project-based collaborations. If you’re running an R&D-heavy product or need a pipeline of junior devs, Chiang Mai can quietly deliver.

You won’t get the volume of talent you’d find in Bangkok or Saigon, but what’s here is accessible, affordable, and often eager to learn. Combine that with the foreign business community based around the university and coworking spaces, and you’ve got a sustainable hiring loop for lean, skilled teams.

## The Smart Visa Helped Tip the Scales

When I first started looking into basing long-term in Thailand, I went deep into visa research. The new Destination Thailand Visa (DTV) seemed promising at first—five years, multiple entries, no work permit required. But after looking into the fine print, it was clear the DTV wasn’t designed for builders.

You can’t work locally. You can’t open a Thai business bank account. You can’t legally hire or scale anything based inside Thailand. It’s basically a tourism visa for remote freelancers or creators working for foreign clients. Great for short-term flexibility—but not for launching a startup.

What changed the game for me was the **SMART S Visa**. It’s part of Thailand’s Smart Visa program, specifically tailored for tech founders who register Thai companies. With this visa, I was able to open a company, get a legal address, access a work permit without extra paperwork, and hire staff—all without the normal dependency on Thai employee quotas.

The process isn’t exactly simple. It took time, documentation, and guidance. I worked with **Settle in Abroad**, a local firm that specializes in visa and incorporation support for foreign founders in Chiang Mai. They helped me navigate BOI registration, visa filings, and work permit approvals.

If you’re serious about staying and building something local, the SMART Visa is the real option. It’s more work upfront, but it unlocks the ability to operate legally, raise capital, and scale a real business here.

## The Real Challenge: Building Locally

Setting up in Thailand isn’t plug-and-play like it is in Delaware or Singapore. The bureaucracy can be slow. You’ll need a Thai partner if you’re not BOI-backed. And there’s no one-click startup package that just works out of the box.

Finding the right local partner—whether as a shareholder, team lead, or support contact—is one of the most important decisions you’ll make. In my case, it took several months to build trust with the right people. I’ve seen others rush this step and pay for it later.

But once you’re through the setup, the benefits are real. Low burn rate. High-quality life. Time to think and build. I’ve had more creative runway in Chiang Mai than I ever did in North America.

There are trade-offs. You don’t have the same support systems or funding networks you’d find in startup capitals. But you gain independence, control, and a space to work without distraction. If you’re building for the long term, that matters.

Thailand won’t build your startup for you—but it will give you the conditions to do it yourself.

## Conclusion: You Don’t Need to Be in Silicon Valley to Build Something Real

Chiang Mai isn’t the next Silicon Valley—and that’s exactly why it works.

It’s not perfect. The visa process is messy. The startup ecosystem is small. And yes, you’ll run into nomads who are more interested in appearances than output. But if you’re focused, self-driven, and willing to work through the rough edges, Chiang Mai offers something most big cities can’t: room to build without constant pressure to burn or perform.

For me, it was the right place at the right time. The infrastructure is solid. The cost of living is low. The lifestyle is healthy. And most importantly, I’ve been able to ship products, grow my projects, and work with talented people—without draining my savings or compromising my vision.

If you’re a founder looking for a quieter, cheaper, and more independent place to build, Chiang Mai might be worth a look.
